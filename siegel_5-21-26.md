# Reflections on Siegel and the Rationality of Perception

## Introduction

In _The Rationality of Perception_, Susanna Siegel introduces and analyses a problem in the epistemology of perception which she refers to as the problem of hijacked experience. One characteristic form of the problem arises when we consider two claims about the generation of knowledge from sensory inputs.

For any object T and predicate Φ:

(1) That T appears Φ to us in a sensory experience justifies our believing that T is Φ

(2) Our belief that T is Φ can cause T to appear Φ to us in a sensory experience of T

We will call the first claim _phenomenal conservatism_[^1] and the second _cognitive penetration_. Cognitive penetration is an empirical thesis[^2]. Nonetheless, insofar as it is possible in principle, it remains relevant for the _a priori_ analysis of epistemological concepts. Specifically, we arrive at a problem when we take these two claims together with another basic principle of rationality:

(3) A belief B is not justified where the supposed justification itself depends on B.

Where a belief B that T is Φ is justified by an experience E of T as Φ; if E itself depends on B then the justification of B would depend on itself. Therefore, B ends up as both justified (by 2) and not justified (by 3).

The lesson Siegel draws from this is that not all perceptual experiences have the same epistemic power with respect to the propositional content which they purport to justify. Ordinarily, sensory perceptual experience enjoys a baseline epistemic power such that sensory experience with content C justifies believing C. However, cases of hijacked experience show that it is possible for experiences to have their epistemic power _downgraded_ such that their epistemic power falls below the baseline.

In _The Rationality of Perception_, Siegel argues that downgraded experiences do not provide rational grounds for belief[^3]. Beliefs based on downgraded experience are irrational and ought not to be upheld. Siegel claims further that the epistemic fault explaining the downgrading of experience must be an _inferential_ fault. Central to Siegel's analysis is the idea that the problem in cases of hijacked experience is an inappropriate response to information[^4]. And, Siegel argues, insofar as inference is the process by which knowledge is obtained in response to prior information; failures in the generation of knowledge that stem from inappropriate responses to information are best explained in terms violating inferential norms[^5].

In this paper, I will argue that while cases of downgraded experience _do_ represent instances of irrationality; this irrationality cannot be explained in terms of inferential norms. Instead, I will argue that the failure involved is best explained as failing to meet standards of practice. Where we fail to meet those standards, we are blameworthy, even when our failure does not involve any bad inferences on our part. A consequence of this is that certain of our epistemic practices may end up counting as irrational even where no prior beliefs or outlook are implicated. As such, the scope of epistemic downgrade under this model is wider than that proposed by Siegel.

## Inferential Routes to Belief

Consider the following case of an inferential route to belief:

(P) Human activity does not have significant impact on climate

(C) Therefore, the government should not regulate carbon emissions

Here, belief C follows reasonably from belief P. Therefore, insofar as it is rational to believe that P, it is rational to believe that C. Nonetheless, if one is irrational in believing that P, then they are also irrational in believing that C. In other words, the rationality of a belief is co-determined by two distinct factors: the rationality of the inferential process and the rationality of the premise.

Siegel characterizes this co-determination under a metaphor of inheritance: the inferred belief inherits the rationality of the input belief from which it is formed. Under this model, it is clear that the premise must possess the relevant epistemic property in common with the conclusion; such that they are both evaluable under the same rubric. It is because the premise is rational that the conclusion is rational as well, assuming an otherwise appropriate inferential relation connects them.

The basic notion of inference operative here is the idea of inference as a response to information. It produces an epistemic state such that the content of the input state is a reason for believing the content of the output state. It represents a specific kind of movement from one epistemic state to another which is evaluable in terms of rational norms. This kind of movement can be contrasted with non-inferential information response patterns. Siegel considers several characteristic examples of non-inferential responses to information including "mental jogging" where we jump by free association from one state to another and the mere shifting of attention from one fact to another without any conception of one depending on the other for justification[^6]. What is distinctive, then, about specifically inferential responses to information is that the appropriateness of the response is explainable in terms of inferential norms.

Consider the case of a skilled chess player who is initially presented with a chess position. From the factual knowledge about the position of the pieces on the board, they form an evaluative judgment that it is good for black. This movement from a factual epistemic state to an evaluative epistemic state can be categorized as well-founded or ill-founded based on its conformity to the norms of inference: Is the evaluation inferentially supported by the board position?

Notice that there is no requirement in this model that the chess player explicitly engage in an act of reckoning in the formulation of their evaluation. It is sufficient that the success conditions for the formation of the judgement are explainable in terms of inferential norms. The defender of a reckoning based account of inference may wish to introduce an additional condition: The inferer must explicitly reckon that the conclusion follows from some premise in order for their believing the conclusion in response to the premise to count as an inference.

Siegel argues that such a reckoning condition fails to account for a variety of different information responses that ought to be considered forms of inference. She considers various kinds of inferential responses to information that do not involve explicit reckoning. In one representative example, Siegel considers a case in which an observer encounters a complex interaction between a clerk and a customer at a post office and forms a belief that the clerk was "kind"[^7]. Due to the complexity of the interaction and the rapidity of the formed judgement, it is not the case that the observer enters into a conscious mental state with content that such and such features entail the kindness of the clerk. Rather, the evaluation is a reflexive response to various social cues. Nonetheless, argues Siegel, because this is a case of categorization, which is a form of rational activity, it must involve the operation of inference.

More generally, we see in cases like this that specific factors in the input information state serve both causal and normative functions: (1) they cause the epistemic agent to form a belief with the output information as content and (2) the resulting belief counts as knowledge in virtue of those factors. In the case of the post office encounter, the fact that the observer knows that the clerk is kind depends upon observing features of the interaction which can justify the subsequent belief. Therefore, the epistemic process involved is inferential.

## Epistemic Charge and the Rationality of Experience

Siegel explains the transmission of rationality in inferential processes in terms of a property of epistemic states she calls _epistemic charge_[^8]. Like its electrical analogue, epistemic charge has positive and negative valence. All else being equal, an appropriate inferential response to an input epistemic state E generating an output epistemic state F transmits the charge of E to F such that F is positively charged only if E is positively charged[^9].

A consequence of formulating the concept of charge transmission in this way is that it gives a minimal criterion for what sorts of epistemic states may be charged. Namely, once we fix the concept of an inferential process as the response to information constrained by inferential norms, we are able to assign a charge to an epistemic state just in the case that it can function as input and output to such processes.

Consider the following case of hijacked experience discussed by Siegel[^10]:

Many of the first users of microscopes favored preformationism. Some preformationists claimed to see embryos in sperm cells that they examined using a microscope. If the sperm cells they examined really did contain embryos within, an excellent explanation of this fact would be that preformationism is true.

Here, the content of the preformationists' perceptual experience is influenced by their prior belief that sperm cells contain embryos. This experience also serves as input to the subsequent strengthening of that belief. If both these processes, that of cognitive penetration in experience and that of hypothesis confirmation by experience, are explainable under the model of inference just discussed; then this would suggest that at least some experiential states share epistemic charge in common with belief states.

The case of cognitive penetration does conform to the inferential model outlined. The form of the inference can be represented as follows[^11]:

(P1) Sperm cells contain embryos.

(P2) This is a sperm cell.

(C) This contains an embryo.

In this case, C is the content of a perceptual experience rather than a belief. But, insofar as this content appears in response to preexisting beliefs with content P1 and P2, the structure of this response still conforms to an inferential model.

Similarly, the subsequent strengthening of the belief in preformationism via supposed observational confirmation also follows an inferential paradigm[^12]:

(P1) This contains an embryo

(P2) This is a sperm cell

(C) This counts as evidence for the claim that sperm cells contain embryos.

This being the case, we conclude that experiences possess epistemic charge in common with beliefs; since, they too occur as inputs and outputs to inferential processes. Moreover, epistemic charge is a rational property of epistemic states. It represents an evaluation in terms of their well-foundedness (as outputs to inferential processes) and epistemic power (as inputs to inferential processes). Therefore, experiential states can be rationally appraised just like belief states.

## The Demarcation Problem for Inference

In this section, we will argue against Siegel's claim, discussed in the previous section, that the influence of belief on experiential content is best understood as the modulation of an experience's epistemic charge by an underlying inferential process.

In broad terms, we face a problem of demarcation in deciding what sorts of information responses count as inferences. Human physiology is rich with information processing of all kinds. Even where we restrict our attention to just those forms of information processing that generate informational states attributable to the agent and are evaluable on the basis of inferential norms, it is not possible to exclude many cases of non-rational information processing.

Consider the case of a blindfolded observer determining the identity of a fluid by taste. For instance, they drink a sip of fluid in a cup and determine that it is "an iced vanilla latte". In the formation of this judgement, information regarding a whole host of physical properties of the fluid feature as conditions on the formation of the judgment. The success of the final categorization depends on integrating this information in a way that conforms to inferential norms. However, not all of these properties (e.g. the shape characteristics of the aromatic compounds detected by chemoreceptors on taste buds) feature as _reasons_ for the categorization made.

A similar phenomenon is observed in, for instance, proprioception. Studies on phantom sensation in individuals with limb amputation suggest that the determination of limb position in space depends upon pre-existing anatomic information constituting a "body schema"[^13]. Inappropriate proprioceptive sensory content persists in patients where this body schema is no longer accurate due to the amputation. This sensory content is derived from responses to anatomic information in a manner that can be evaluated against inferential norms. However, such information does not feature as a reason for any belief or judgement on the part of the agent. It is unclear how to exclude this non-rational information on a purely response based account of inference.

## Inference and the First-Person Perspective

The issue of demarcation highlights a basic problem in the distinction between reasons and causes. It is because actions emanate from reasons that an action is attributable to an agent as intentional and freely undertaken. A part of their role in underwriting an agent's freedom is that reasons are endorsed by the agent. In this sense, they form a part of the agent's _own_ perspective on their actions. This is the core difference between a reason and a mere cause.

Consider a case of two individuals engaged in a physical altercation. When asked why Alice punched Bob, an answer in terms of the alcohol content of Alice's blood may be causally explanatory. But, it does not reflect her reasons for her action. That reasons must be attributable to the agent is a part of what distinguishes reasons from causes, but it is not the whole story. For instance, reference to personality traits such as Alice's drinking habits, while more directly attributable to the agent, still does not constitute an explanation in terms of Alice's reasons.

Reasons are distinct from actions in being attributable to the agent specifically as the way in which _they_ would explain their actions. Reasons justify. They serve a social function. Our reasons are how we may justify ourselves to our peers and to ourselves. They represent our perspective.

Without a first-person perspective, there is no meaningful demarcation between causal and rational explanation. Any physical property involved causally in producing an action can-in principle-count as a reason just insofar as it is epistemically accessible to the agent and endorsed by them as a reasonable basis for that action. In the above case, if the patient knew their own blood alcohol level concentration and conceived of this as a rational basis for engaging in violent action; then, this in itself would qualify it as a reason. That such information is in fact not a typical part of an agent's own perspective on their actions is precisely why we are apt to consider it a mere cause and not a reason.

What then to make of Siegel's cases of inference without reckoning? The answer is that explicit reckoning is not necessary for some bit of information to be considered a part of a person's first-person perspective. Consider again the case of categorizing a clerk at the post office as "kind" where various features of a social interaction act as cues for evaluation. Here, there is no explicit act of reckoning. However, the cueing features may still participate in first-personal explanations of the judgement insofar as we expect the observer to endorse them as reasons. Contrast this with the case of the preformationist. It may be the case that their prior conviction that sperm cells contain embryos influences their perceptual experience during microscopy. But this conviction would not feature as part of the preformationist's own perspective in justifying why they saw what they saw. As such, an inferential model does not serve in explaining the ill-foundedness of the preformationist's experiential content or subsequent strengthening of their conviction.

## Inferential Modulation, Even If True, Cannot Explain all Cases of Epistemic Downgrade

Moreover, even if we accept inferential modulation as the explanation for epistemic downgrading; this analysis does not cover all instances where epistemic norms are violated by an agent in forming a belief based on an experience.

Consider a physician examining a patient with a clot in a deep leg vein. The physician fails to notice a size discrepancy between the patient's legs and rules out a clot as the cause of the patient's symptoms. Now, let us suppose that the physician's failure to notice a size discrepancy stemmed from improper technique in the examination. Here, in the inferential move from perceiving no size discrepancy in the limbs to believing that there is no clot, the ill-foundedness of the diagnosis is specifically due to its inheriting the ill-foundedness of the perception. In such a case, the physician's belief that the patient does not have a blood clot is ill-justified by their examination. However, the examination's lack of epistemic power cannot be explained in terms of modulation by prior outlooks. Rather, its power is downgraded because of a failure in specifically _observational_ norms: the physician failed to properly examine the patient.

## Epistemic Fault as a Failure to Meet Standard of Practice

How do we understand the epistemic downgrading of experience from violating observational rather than inferential norms?

One possible solution is to fall back to a form of externalism; for instance, a form of process reliabilism. Under reliabilism, an epistemic process has epistemic power to ground a belief just insofar as the process has a high truth-to-falsity ratio in generating belief[^14]. Such an account would be able to explain why even true beliefs formed on the basis of unreliable observation might fail to count as knowledge. However, it is unable to provide the kind of explanation necessary to justify normative evaluations of the agent.

The case of medical diagnostic failure is instructive. Contrast the initial case of diagnostic failure due to bad examination technique with a case where the physician correctly performs the examination but simply fails to recognize a size discrepancy due to its subtlety. Here, the diagnostic failure can be explained by the unreliability of their examination. But, supposing that most physicians would fail to notice a discrepancy of this magnitude, it is not clear that the physician was being irrational.

What is needed, then, is an account of epistemic failure that preserves the normative import required for the attribution of blame to the agent. That is, we need an account of the failure in terms of their failing to meet observational _norms_. In the above cases of diagnostic failure, the physician is blameworthy where their examination involved poor technique. However, the physician is not blameworthy where they failed to diagnose due to a reasonable limitation in their sense faculties.

The difference between the two cases is that, although both physicians lacked knowledge due to unreliable examinations, only the first physician violated the _standards of practice_. Violation of the standards of practice is the violation of a norm. It specifies the conditions under which an epistemic agent is blameworthy for failure. Moreover, standards of practice extend to aspects of a person's observational practices even where the explanation for why the observation was unreliable is purely causal in nature. As such, their applicability is broader than a purely inferential model of rationality. Applied to Siegel's case of the preformationist, we can see that the irrationality of their microscopic confirmation of preformationism stems from their being subject to confirmation bias. And insofar as confirmation bias represents an investigative failing on their part as researchers, the observational failure is attributable to them. In this case, the involvement of inference need play no role. Nonetheless, the normative force required for explaining epistemic downgrade is preserved.

## Conclusion

In summary, we have shown that Siegel's analysis of perceptual hijacking correctly places accountability on epistemic agents. Moreover, we have argued that the form of epistemic appraisal involved allows for the treatment of experiences and beliefs on equal footing as rational states. However, we have argued that the basis for assignment of epistemic value to experiences differs from the basis for assignment to beliefs. In particular, whereas the formation of beliefs on the basis of prior information is accountable to inferential norms; the formation of experiences as a result of observational practices is accountable to specifically observational norms. In addition, we have argued that the correct way to understand observational norms is in terms of standards of practice.
