# A Diṅnāgian Critique of Phenomenal Concepts

Our first-order knowledge of such things as the events we witness or the properties of objects we encounter generally submits to a twofold description; from a first-person and third-person point of view. Think about an experience that elicits strong feelings—a bit of music, for instance, that is particularly disliked. If a friend asks *why* it was so unpleasant, the sort of description one would offer naturally involves reference to the music as it manifests itself to *them*: "The music was horribly mixed; it was shrill, loud, almost painful to hear".

In response to a question about our reasons for doing something or thinking a certain way, adopting a first-person stance seems a matter of necessity. But, taken for a description of the musical experience as *object*, it is liable to inspire a certain unease. In his paper, *What is it Like to be a Bat*, Thomas Nagel diagnoses the source of this unease as the first-person description's failure to be robustly inter-subjective [pg 439]. To see this, Nagel invites us to reflect on the way the world might appear from the perspective of a creature, such as a bat, that is radically different from us in its cognitive and sensory constitution—to consider, in his words, what it is *like* to inhabit such a perspective.

Understanding what it is like to be a bat demands a description of bat experience that eschews purely functional or causal concepts. While the mental states of a bat may certainly play a functional rule in its biology and have causal significance in explaining its behaviors, descriptions involving effects and functions do not in themselves address questions about what it is like to *be* the sort of thing that has that mental state; i.e. questions about its phenomenology [pg 437]. But, what *would* an answer to this question look like? Insofar as bat biology—with its distinctive sensory and cognitive machinery—differs radically from our own; an answer to this question purely in terms of phenomenological categories that we ourselves are acquainted with would seem just as much to leave something out. Such a description may tell us what it would be like for *us* to be a bat, but not necessarily what it would be like for the *bat* [pg 439].

This seems to leave us with something of an epistemic gap: If a first-person description of bat experience must include some elements that are neither (1) objects with which we are acquainted, nor (2) definable in terms of their causal or functional relationship to such things; then, it would seem like we have no epistemic access to such knowledge at all. As Nagel puts it, knowledge about bat neurophysiology is fundamentally different from knowledge of bat phenomenology because the latter is "accessible only from one point of view" [442]. But the implication of this seems to be stronger than just a problem of epistemic access: phenomenological questions are such that not only are we unable answer them, but we are seemingly unable to even *comprehend* what an answer might look like. For, in addition to lacking the resources to verify the truth or falsity of such descriptions, we lack the requisite concepts for understanding such descriptions, in the first place. It is in this sense that phenomenological knowledge is *subjective*. Phenomenological facts are not just metaphysically dependent on a subject—this, after all, would not necessarily debar objective description. Rather, its subjectivity consists in the fact that descriptions of phenomenological facts can be made intelligible only to certain types of subjects and not others.

The subjectivity of phenomenological knowledge raises significant concerns for the possibility of a unified theory, reductive or otherwise, that treats consciousness on par with knowledge about objects—the possibility, that is, for a science of consciousness. Consider the following issue: if the project of science is to arrive at a comprehensive understanding of nature; insofar as consciousness is a natural phenomenon, we should expect to have a general theory of consciousness. But, if certain facts about consciousness are unknowable, in principle, to certain subjects—then, a general theory of consciousnesses is impossible, *in principle*. Because, for such subjects—subjects, that is, like us—any given theory would fail to account for at least some facts about the world; i.e., those facts for which that subject lacks the requisite conceptual apparatus.

We can sharpen this dichotomy between "scientific" and "phenomenological" knowledge by appealing to the old Rusellian distinction between acquaintance and description. In his paper, *Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description*, Bertrand Russel argues that there are two modes of epistemic access available to us. Our ordinary discursive knowledge of facts—the sorts of things we might find in a biology textbook—are what he calls descriptive knowledge. Our knowledge of objects as, for instance, the referents of proper names, he calls *acquaintance*. That we have descriptive knowledge is evident from our capacity to engage in linguistic communication and "book-learning"; but, the notion of acquaintance may seem a bit mysterious. Russell argues that we need such a notion of the direct knowledge of objects because otherwise we would be unable to make sense of how description gives us knowledge. Gaining knowledge from a description depends on prior knowledge of the meanings of the terms and connectives present in that description. Although knowledge of the meanings of terms could itself proceed by description—i.e., via definition—this must eventually bottom out, on pain of regress, to a form of knowledge that is unmediated by language. Such direct knowledge of an object as the referent of a term is what Russel calls "acquaintance". As he puts it, "all propositions intelligible to us, whether or not they primarily concern things only known to us by description, are composed wholly of constituents with which we are acquainted, for a constituent with which we are not acquainted is *unintelligible to us*" [128, emph mine].

What is distinctive about the objects of scientific description is that while access to them depends on acquaintance with *some* other object, it does not depend on acquaintance with any one object in particular. Rather, because they are known through, e.g., their causal interactions with other physical phenomena, scientific phenomena can be described in terms of their relationship to a wide range of possible objects of acquaintance. This is why, for instance, both a human and an alien scientist could have scientific knowledge of the same set of objects—from macroscopic items like rocks to fundamental particles and fields—despite potentially knowing them through acquaintance with radically different sorts of sense data and concepts. This is what makes such objects robustly inter-subjective. The problem with consciousness is that any causal or functional properties that states of consciousness have seem to be logically independent of their qualitative character. If such states are logically dependent on anything, it seems it could only be other qualitative properties. This being the case, there does not seem to be a way to have knowledge by description of what it is *like* to be in such states without already having acquaintance with related phenomenological properties. That is, it does not seem to be possible to offer a purely "third-person" description of such states. This is the familiar "hard problem" of consciousnesses [ ].

The possibility of a science of consciousness, then, seems to hinge on breaking down the dependence of descriptive knowledge on acquaintance. To this effect: in his anthology *Essays on the Theory of Knowledge* [Pramāṇasamuccayaḥ], the 6th ce Indian philosopher Diṅnāga develops an argument concerning the relationship—or lack there of—between knowledge gained through through the direct apprehension of objects [pratyakṣam] and knowledge gained through the understanding of sentences [śābdaḥ] (&#x2026;). This argument pivots around a semantic analysis of phenomenological descriptions as being built out of terms expressing primitive conceptual categories [jātiśabdāḥ], assembled together using logical connectives and operators. For instance, a description of music as "shrill, loud, and unpleasant" can be analyzed as the conjunction of 'shrill', 'loud', and the negation of 'pleasant'. Our ability to understand such descriptions, in turn, depends on our possessing the concepts through which we can recognize certain experiences as having the character of "shrillness", "loudness", etc. 

The *primitiveness* of these concepts lies in their independence from one another. This is not necessarily a logical independence; since, the applicability of one concept to an experience can entail the applicability or in-applicability of another. Instead, their independence is an epistemic one: possession of a given primitive concept is not entailed by possession of any set of other primitive concepts.

The epistemic independence of first-person concepts lies in apparent tension with the fact that we observe many instances where a single experience can simultaneously conceptualized under two or more different primitive first-person concepts. For example, one and the same audible tone can be conceptualized as "shrill" and "loud". One way to make sense of this is by understanding concept possession as being based on acquaintance not with just a single property but with a complex condition involving potentially conjunctive and disjunctive parts.

We can state this condition formally. First, let us define the following relations:

1.  Pxy means that subject *x* possess the concept *y*
2.  Kxy means that subject *x* has knowledge by acquainted of the property *y*
3.  Ixy means that experience *x* can be conceptualized under concept *y*
4.  Hxy means that experience *x* has the property *y*
5.  Ascq for some subject *s*, concept *c* and set of properties *q* is defined as:
    Ascq ⟺ ∀p∈q. Ksp ∧ [ ∀e. Iec ⇔ Hep ]
6.  Bscr for some subject *s*, concept *c* and set of sets of properties r is defined as:
    Bscr ⟺ [ ∀q∈r,p∈q. Ksp ] ∧ [ ∀e,∃q∈r,∀p∈q. Iec ⇔ Hep ]

Now, let us suppose that for any subject *s* and concept *c*: K(s, c) iff at least one of the two conditions are met:

**Premise 1**
A. ∃q. Ascq
B. ∃r. Bscr

The predicates A, and B represent different kinds of application conditions (pravr̥ttinimittāni) for concepts. In a *Type A* condition, a concept applies to an experience only if it satisfies a certain conjuctive condition involving properties with which a given subject must already be acquainted; whereas, a *Type B* condition involves a disjunction of *Type A* subconditions. What is notably missing is a condition involving negation. This is because unlike in the case of a conjuction of properties, which we can be acquainted with via acquantaince with its constituents, we cannot have knowledge by acquaintance of the *absence* of a property.

**Premise 1** represents a general constraint on any Russellian account of concept possession; that is, any account where the intelligibility of a concept depends on acquaintance with the elements that condition its applicability. To show that such accounts are, in general, untenable; we proceed by successively demonstrating the untenability of each alternative 1-3.

The idea again involves instances of the simultaneous conceptualization of an experience under two different concepts. Specifically, we will consider the case when a single phenomenal quality is conceptualized at different levels of granularity.

Take the case of a subject's experience of color. Even an untrained eye can readily distinguish, e.g., blue from green. But, subtler distinctions may pass beneath their notice. With training, the same individual presented with colors of slightly different hue may begin to distinguish two or three categories where they previously saw all of them as a single color. Notice, however, that when a subject acquires a more granular concept it is not necessary that they lose the less granular concept. Even when they can recognize the difference between, e.g. teal and turquoise, they are still able to conceptualize these same colors as just blue.

What does this mean for the epistemology of first-person concepts? If we suppose that there are only Type A conditions then trying to explain the co-application of two concepts that differ only in degree of granularity becomes difficult. We cannot take the application conditions of two such concepts, e.g. 'blue' and 'teal', to involve identical properties, because then the concepts would be synonymous; there would be no case where 'blue' and 'teal' fail to co-apply (&#x2026;). On the other hand, they properties involved cannot be completely different, because then we cannot account for the fact that for any experience being 'teal' necessarily implies being 'blue'.

One possibility is to consider the properties involved in the application conditions of 'blue' to be a proper subset of those in 'teal'. This would allow us explain the necessary logical relationship between higher and lower granularity concepts. But it would run into another problem. Insofar as we must be acquainted with the properties involved in the application conditions for the concepts that we possess (i.e Ascp → Ksp), it should be possible to isolate in attention the properties in 'teal' that make it generically 'blue' from those that make it 'teal' in particular. However, we can neither divide in our attention the quality of a simple color experience into parts nor even imagine what focusing on a mere 'blueness' would be like. The very concept of the generic blueness, separate from its identity as a particular hue, is not even intelligible. This principle, which the post-Diṅnāgian philosopher Dharmakīrti calls *The Co-Apprehension Principle* (sahopalambaniyamaḥ) provides a criteria for deciding when the application of distinct concepts to an experience involves acquaintance with distinct properties and when this is just a form of abstraction (&#x2026;).

In order to save an acquaintance based account of concept possession, we could suppose that the less granular concept is actually disjunctive in form. So, the application of the concept 'blue' to a 'teal' experience is conditioned by a different set of properties then its application to a 'turquoise' experience. The problem with this proposal is that, because the possession of a concept necessitates prior acquaintance with all the properties involved in its application condition, we would not be able to apply a disjunctive concept to a subcategory which we have not yet encountered. For example, we could not apply the concept 'blue' to a shade of blue that we have not yet encountered. Yet, it is at least possible that a subject possessing the concepts 'blue' and having prior acquaintance with teal and turquoise experience, being presented for the first time with some intermediate shade, would apply the concept 'blue' to it. And, insofar as this would be a true description of that shade—an intermediate shade, between teal and turquoise is still blue—this would imply that they had could correctly apply the 'blue' concept to particular experiential qualities that they have not previously encountered. This observation, which Diṅnāga calls the *openendedness* [ānantyam] of conceptual categories, precludes the possibility that primitive concepts can be disjunctive (&#x2026;).

As such, neither a *Type A* nor a *Type B* condition can explain our possession of primitive first-person concepts such as 'blue' which can be re-conceptualized at higher granularity. And since this exhausts the possibility of an acquaintance based account (**Premise 1**) of concept possession, we must conclude that the intelligibility of first-person descriptions does not depend on prior acquaintance with the form of experience being described. Diṅnāga's critique of subjectivity cuts two ways. One the one hand, it means that there is nothing essential about bat experience that precludes us from understanding what it is like to be a bat. On the other hand, it means that we do not enjoy any sort of privileged vantage point with regards to understanding either the experience of other humans, or even our own. There is nothing special about the first-person stance.
