# Reflections on Rawlsian Public Reason Theories

## Introduction

In Justice as Fairness: A Restatement, John Rawls develops and defends a procedural conception of justice as opposed to a conception of the fair allocation of goods as explaining the basic structure of justice in democratic society[^1]. Under a procedural conception of justice, the basic structure of society is organized on the basis of publicly recognized rules which govern the functioning of political institutions and determine the rights and obligations people have with respect to these institutions and each other. Once the rules are adopted, people are free to transact as they wish within their purview. Questions of justice are adjudicated not with reference to their actual outcomes but with reference to the rules that govern the basic structure of society[^2].

This basic structure is minimal. It does not answer all questions of morality but is strictly oriented towards the public justification of political claims[^3]. Claims, that is, which have to do with the terms of cooperation between free and equal citizens. In a pluralistic society, citizens may reason ethically about and evaluate actions on the basis of differing conceptions of the good and with respect to different moral doctrines and sources of authority. However, insofar as they are concerned with justifying their political claims to each other, it is necessary for these diverse people to share some overlapping consensus on issues pertaining to the adjudication of social disagreements in a public space[^4].

As a theory of public justification, a procedural conception of justice offers two closely connected advantages over a conception of justice as fair allocation. First, it is possible to reason about a procedural conception of justice abstractly. Second, the behaviour of actual citizens and institutions in concrete situations can be judged against their conformity to the procedure insofar as the procedure can be justified to them a priori. We might compare this to the rules of a game. Once the rules of the game are specified, the players are held accountable to the results of the game, whatsoever they might end up being, just so long as the rules were acceptable at the onset. In the same way, insofar as the rules of social cooperation are a priori acceptable, the resulting outcomes must also be considered acceptable, whatever they may end up being[^5].

The procedural conception of justice is a theory of public reason. It seeks to navigate a middle road between two opposing accounts of the basis of evaluation for public claims[^6]. On one side, we have a contractual conception of the basis for adjudicating public disagreements. Here, the operative idea is that parties to the disagreement actually consent to the principles of adjudication prior to the result being decided. On the other side, we have an objective conception of the basis for adjudication[^7]. Here, the disagreement is adjudicated with reference to some external, "objective" standard taken as moral or political truth[^8]. Rawls' procedural conception of justice asks, instead, whether the political principles adopted are justifiable to the parties involved, irrespective of their actual consent, based upon a minimal conception of the type of individuals to which these principles are addressed. In particular, based on our conception of the parties involved as free and equal persons[^9].

Rawls' account is constrained by two conditions which we will call, following Thrasher (2019), construction and representation[^10]. By construction, we mean that the principles underlying procedural justice are generated by an abstract model of negotiation where appropriately specified model representatives of actual individuals decide on the principles in question[^11]. Principles so constructed do not depend for their justification on reference to an objective standard of moral or political truth. Rather, they draw their justification from their being agreeable to the individuals to whom they are addressed. The modal quality of the word "agreeable" is important here. It is not necessary that the individuals actually agree to the proposed principles. Rather, they are agree-able because the parties in the model of negotiation represent the individuals to which the principles are addressed. The property of representation, then, can be understood as the relation between model individuals and actual individuals such that the agreement of the former has normative import for the latter[^12].

To summarize, theories of public reason offer a middle ground between contractual and objective standards for the public adjudication of political claims. Under such an account, individuals can be held accountable to certain principles of justice where:

- Those principles are constructed via an abstract procedure involving an appropriately specified model of negotiation (construction).
- The hypothetical parties involved in the model represent the actual individuals to which the principles are addressed (representation).

In order for a system of procedural justice to function as a theory of public reason, it is necessary that there exist a specification of the model and an account of the representation relation such that both the construction and representation conditions are satisfied.

In this paper, I will argue that it not possible to simultaneously satisfy both the construction and representation conditions under Rawls' conception of the model of negotiation and representation relation. As such, I will argue that a procedural conception of justice so construed is not a tenable account of the basic structure of justice in society. In particular, I will argue that it is not possible to adjudicate political claims on the basis of procedural principles whose justification does not depend on objective standards of moral evaluation or empirical knowledge about the actual contexts in which these claims arise.

## The Original Position and The Veil of Ignorance

In Justice as Fairness, Rawls introduces the original position as the appropriate model for constructing the principles of justice. The original position models a scenario where a set of individuals gather to negotiate the fair terms of cooperation for members of a given society. In these negotiations, each individual considers themselves as representing certain members of the society whose constitution they have assembled to decide. However, the negotiating parties do not know precisely which members of that society form their constituents. They are, as Rawls puts it, behind a "veil of ignorance".

The device the Veil of Ignorance serves two distinct goals. First, it addresses the ahistoricity of social contract theories. It is in fact not the case that we agree to the laws or constitution of our society or the principles that inform reasoned discourse prior to our being beholden to them-as in our signing a contract at the onset of joining an organization. Rather, we simply find ourselves in society with pre-existing legal and political structures. The question then, as Rawls reminds us in the opening to Justice as Fairness, is whether we can reconcile ourselves to these structures[^13]. This is captured by the modal framing of the question. Would I, freely and in good conscience, accept these principles as the political constitution of my society?

Second, the veil of ignorance addresses the issues of freedom and conscience. In actual contractual negotiations, individuals are not entirely free in their decision making. Rather, their freedom is constrained by relations of power between the involved parties[^14]. Those with more power can leverage that power to extract concessions from their less advantaged co-participants which the latter would not assent to if they were not so disadvantaged. Additionally, where the terms of a contract confer advantage to one party over another, individuals may negotiate for their own advantage over the interests of others.

By removing this negotiation from the actual world and situating it in the original position, Rawls is able to control for disparities of power and concern amongst the parties involved in the negotiation. Since individuals behind the veil of ignorance do not know whom they represent in the actual world they cannot select a priori for a set of principles that serve the interests of their constituents over others.

Nonetheless, the concept of the original position shares with social contract theory in tying the normative force of the principles of justice to their agreement by the individuals whose behaviour it constrains. One is accountable to the principles of justice because and only insofar as they would agree to those principles in the original position[^15]:

Immediately the question arises as to how the fair terms of cooperation are specified. For example: Are they specified by an authority distinct from the persons cooperating, say, by God's law? Or are these terms recognized by everyone as fair by reference to a moral order of values, say, by rational intuition, or by reference to what some have viewed as "natural law"? Or are they settled by an agreement reached by free and equal citizens engaged in cooperation, and made in view of what they regard as their reciprocal advantage, or good? Justice as fairness adopts a form of the last answer: the fair terms of social cooperation are to be given by an agreement entered into by those engaged in it.

This is necessary for Rawls because a plurality of perspectives, moral understandings, and conceptions of the good are a natural and inevitable feature of a liberal democratic society. In such a society, where reason, rather than coercive power, is used to negotiate disagreements, it cannot be that people must be made to agree to a specific moral framework against their will. Moreover, it is not clear that consensus can be reached on which moral framework to adopt based on reason alone. Therefore, it cannot be the case that individuals must be answerable to a principle of justice which derives its normative force from its standing withing a given comprehensive moral framework. Rather, they must be themselves arrived at deliberatively. In a pluralistic society, individuals can be held accountable to only those principles of justice that are justifiable to them.

## The Problem of Specification

Recall that Rawls' public reason theory involves constructing the principles of justice out of an abstract model of negotiation. Under such an account, we require a specification of the model strong enough to (1) guarantee that parties to the negotiation under the model reach a consensus on which principles to adopt and (2) we can deduce which principles the parties would decide on from an analysis of the model specification. Additionally, the specification must ensure that the model individuals represent actual individuals, as discussed. Rawls then supplies the veil of ignorance as a constraint on the decision procedure, as discussed in the previous section. However, while an important element of Rawls specification of the model for negotiation, the veil of ignorance is not sufficient on its own to guarantee that a consensus will be reached.

Consider a set of individuals which are radically polarized such that (1) they can be subdivided into groups: 'A' and 'B', where (2) for any pairwise choice, if a member of set 'A' selects one choice, a member of set 'B' would select the other. Again, such a set of individuals seems at least possible. And it is clear that could be no consensus among such individuals even in the original position.

Conversely, we can ensure consensus is reached by stipulating that each individual in the model is a dispositional clone. That is, all differences in the behaviour of these individuals are explainable in terms of differences where they stand in their environment. And, insofar as all individuals in the original position are symmetrically situated by definition; we would expect members of a society of dispositional clones to always arrive at a consensus in the original position. In such a case, however, the problem of representation becomes particularly acute.

## The Problem of Representation

Under a naive conception of the representation relation as, e.g., dispositional identity-where the model individual would represent an actual individual just insofar as the represented individual would behave the same as the model individual under the same conditions-then clearly a model where all parties are dispositional clones would fail to represent citizens of a pluralistic society. However, the relation of representation does not need to be as strong as this naive conception in order to secure the normative force required.

Consider, for instance, the case of representation in medical ethics. When a patient is unable to provide consent for a medical procedure, an appointed agent via a durable power of attorney can make decisions on their behalf. When there is no DPOA, states may have procedures for appointing a patient surrogate. In either case, the surrogate represents the patient such that the consent of the surrogate can be substituted for the consent of the patient. In such a case, procedures done to the patient can be justified on the bases of the surrogate's consent such that if the patient subsequently regains the ability to make decisions, they cannot make a claim of consent-breach against the medical team regarding the procedure made while they were incapacitated.

In medical ethic and legal literature, the three predominant standards for surrogacy are known wishes, substituted judgement and best interest[^16]. For instance, the California Probate Code §4714 states[^17]:

A surrogate, including a person acting as a surrogate, shall make a health care decision in accordance with the patient's individual health care instructions, if any, and other wishes to the extent known to the surrogate. Otherwise, the surrogate shall make the decision in accordance with the surrogate's determination of the patient's best interest. In determining the patient's best interest, the surrogate shall consider the patient's personal values to the extent known to the surrogate.

Here, the known wishes standard means following the explicit instructions laid down by the patient prior to their incapacitation. Where these are unknown, the standard of substituted judgement comes into play. Here, the basis for decision making involves inferring what the patient's decision would have been based on a knowledge of the patient's wishes with regards to similar situations as well as knowledge of their values and conceptions of the good. Here, the idea is of the surrogate making a judgement based on what they believe the patient would choose if they were able to make the decision themselves. When the surrogate has no specific knowledge of the patient's wishes or values, then the best interest standard allows for what the surrogate believes is in the patient's best interest (regardless of what the patient would themselves choose) to be the standard of consent in this case.

Clearly, the known wishes standard cannot apply to the parties in the original position, since this is prohibited by the veil of ignorance. It also appears that the substituted decision making standard could not apply behind the veil of ignorance. Because the parties in the original position do not know any specific information about the actual citizens they represent, they have no basis for determining what specifically their clients would judge to be best over the judgements of any other individual. Insofar as different individuals may judge which of two opposing principles of justice is preferrable, it would not be possible for the parties to the original position to know which preference would be favored by their clients over others.

The best interest standard appears, then, to be the only rational basis for representation in the original position. The parties to the original position can represent actual individual only insofar as the decisions of those parties represent the best interests of the citizens. Note, however, that the best interest standard involves the parties making judgments of best interest on the basis of the conceptions of the good held by the parties. This means the model must include as a part of its specification a conception of the good appropriate to be applied to citizens as the basis of judgements of their best interest. However, this conflicts with the construction constraint on public reason theories. Recall that the construction constraint prohibits reliance for the specification of the principles of justice on any objective standards of moral or political truth. In this case, stipulating a priori specific goods as the appropriate bases for judging what is in the best interests of the represented individuals would constitute a breach of this prohibition. After all, the stipulated goods cannot be justified by the model itself, since it is presupposed by the model. And they cannot be justified by something outside the model, since this would involve an external standard of moral truth.

## Substituted Judgement Behind the Veil of Ignorance

Rawls suggests a way for a minimal form of the substituted judgment standard to apply behind the veil of ignorance. He begins by noting that the model of political deliberation involved in the construction of the principles of justice must be sufficiently broad to provide for these principles a certain kind of generality[^18]:

It is a commonplace of moral philosophy to require first principles to be general and universal. Principles are general when it is possible to state them without the use of proper names or rigged definite descriptions. They are universal when they can be applied without inconsistency or self-defeating incoherence to all moral agents, in our case, to all citizens in the society in question.

Nonetheless, it is not the case that the model must represent just any sort of individual whatsoever. Rather, the model is a model of public reason. It is a model whereby members of a society deliberate on the basis for the negotiation of disagreements and the justification of political claims against the members and institutions of that society. People in different kinds of societies-such as monarchies, theocracies, ethnonationalist regimes, or anarchist communes-may deliberate from different principles of public reason. What we are interested in, then, is a model of public reason representative of a particular kind of society. That is, we are specifically those principles of justice that enable deliberation in liberal democracies. As Rawls explains[^19]:

Recall first that justice as fairness is framed for a democratic society. Its principles are meant to answer the question: once we view a democratic society as a fair system of social cooperation between citizens regarded as free and equal, what principles are most appropriate to it?

The underlying commitment here is to an analysis of citizens conceived of as free and equal. It is to those sort of citizens, and not others, to which these principles of justice are addressed. Therefore, it is only those sorts of individuals that the model parties must represent. That is to say, Rawls understands a liberal democracy as diverse people cooperating freely and fairly with one another for mutual advantage. The principles of justice that inform discourse in such a society must respect the autonomy of persons, recognize the equal significance of the interests of each person, and have space for diverse points of view and systems of value informing each individual in their pursuit of their own intended ends.

Rawls conceives of freedom and equality in terms of the citizens' endowment with two basic "moral powers"; the capacity to (1) "understand, to apply, and to act from…the principles of political justice", and (3) "have, to revise, and rationally pursue a conception of the good"[^20]. Here, it is not stipulated that individuals all share the same conception of the good or affirm a single comprehensive doctrine specifying all the dimensions of justice and ethical conduct in society. Rather, it is simply that they are sensitive to moral and political reasoning and the normative commitments that stem from them.

Rawls' idea, then, is to use this conception of society as constituted by free and equal citizens to allow for a minimal form of the substituted judgement standard to apply to the model parties in the original position. While the parties are not able to ask what judgments their particular clients would prefer; nonetheless, they insofar as they know that their clients are fair and equal citizens, thy are able to ask how an individual would rank these preferences given that they conceive of themselves as free and equal citizens of a liberal democracy[^21].

Since the veil of ignorance prevents the parties from knowing the (comprehensive) doctrines and conceptions of the good of the persons they represent, they must have some other grounds for deciding which principles to select in the original position. Here we face a serious problem: unless we can set up the original position so that the parties can agree on principles of justice moved by appropriate grounds, justice as fairness cannot be carried through. To solve this problem is one reason we introduced the idea of primary goods and enumerated a list of items falling under this heading (§17.2). As we saw, these goods are identified by asking which things are generally necessary as social conditions and all-purpose means to enable citizens, regarded as free and equal, adequately to develop and fully exercise their two moral powers, and to pursue their determinate conceptions of the good.

Here, the device of a list of primary goods serves as the basis for preference selection in the original position. While this list features in the specification of the model, it does not breach the construction constrain on models of public reason because it follows from the conception of citizens as fair and equal. Since this conception merely constrains the scope of which individuals are accountable to the principles constructed by the model, it does not involve reference to an external standard of moral truth.

## The Problem of Value Gaps

The problem with using a list of primary goods as the basis for preference selection under a substituted judgment standard of representation in a model of public reason is that while the list represents some of the conceptions of the good involved in the decision making of the represented individuals, it does not represent what that individual would choose in a given decision task. This is because, the gaps in the list of values may make just as substantive a contribution to the decision making of an individual as the values that are present in the list.

Consider another case of legal surrogacy in medicine. A patient is found unconscious on a city street and brought into the hospital by ambulance. On initial evaluation, they are found to be severely anaemic and in need of emergent blood transfusion. However, the patient is incapacitated and has no DPOA or medical advanced directive. A next of kin is identified and appointed surrogate in accordance with local law. However, the patient and surrogate are estranged and the surrogate is not sure of the particular preferences or values of the patient. Nonetheless, the surrogate does know at least that the patient values their life and health and would wish for life saving treatment measures if offered. Given this knowledge, the surrogate consents on the patient's behalf for blood transfusion.

Now, let us suppose that the patient does value their life and health. However, unbeknownst to the surrogate, they are part of a religious group that believes blood transfusions are proscribed. Here, the religious value is a gap in the list of values that the surrogate used to make a substituted judgement. This value gap represents a significant omission that profoundly impacts the decision making of the patient. For, although they do value their life and health, they would have declined the blood transfusion due to their conflicting religious convictions.

One response is that while value gaps may cause the decision making of an individual to deviate from the judgement of the representative; nonetheless, since the substituted judgment standard only stipulates that the representative judge according to what they believe the patient would choose on the basis of their best knowledge, this deviation does not breach the substituted judgement standard.

While a judgment made on this basis would represent the surrogate's best judgement of the patient's preferences, where the knowledge of the surrogate does not extend to certain central values that play an essential role in forming the patient's ethical outlook, it seems unclear to what extent the patient can be held accountable to the surrogate's judgements as representative of theirs. In particular, where the purpose of the representation relation is to see that the judgments of the representative are justifiable to the represented party; it seems that where there is a significant value gap between the represented party and the basis for judgment by the representative, their judgment would fail to be justifiable to the represented party.

The temporal constraints on medical decision making may require that the surrogate make decisions which are not revisable after the patient regains their faculties. As such, it is reasonable that the patient is unable to make a claim against the medical team where the consent provided by a legal surrogate did not align with the patient. However, where the surrogate is making decisions to which the client is held accountable even after they have regained their autonomy, as in the case of the model of the original position constructing principles to which citizens are accountable in their subsequent free actions, this problem of alignment in the face of value gaps becomes more acute.

Moreover, where the representative lacks all specific knowledge of the organizing values in their clients' ethical and political lives, the substituted judgment standard fails to meaningfully distinguish itself from the best interest standard. Since, the two would generate the same preference ordering and deviate in all the same ways from the actual preferences of the particular individuals they purport to represent.

In such a case, the list of primary goods comes to impose an asymmetry between conceptions of the good that make it on the list and those that do not. Only the former come to have political significance, whereas the latter are relegated to a secondary status and are given no organizing role in the formation of the principles of justice. Where this asymmetry fails to align with the ranking of values endorsed by actual individuals, the imposition of the asymmetry on them amounts to the deployment of an external standard in the evaluation of which values ought to feature in the justification of political claims and in the formulation of its principles. As such, a primary goods-based standard of judgement either fails to represent due to nonalignment with the represented party or fails to be constructed due to its imposition of an ordering of values on to citizens that may not accept it.

## Conclusion

In this paper, we have argued that a Rawlsian public reason theory involving a model of negotiation where parties remain behind a veil of ignorance cannot simultaneously satisfy the construction and representation constraints on such theories. Because the parties to negotiation in the model are ignorant of the particularities regarding the values and ethical conceptions of actual individuals, the model fails to represent society and its citizens. In particular, the political principles generated by the model would not be justifiable to the individuals to which those principles are addressed where certain central and organizing values of the individuals are missing in the list of primary goods adopted for specifying the model. In such a case, Rawls' theory of justice either fails to represent actual citizens or covertly depends on an objective standard for selecting what conceptions of the good matter for deciding the principles of justice and what are irrelevant to it.
