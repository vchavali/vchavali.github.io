Why study non-canonical early modern philosophers

Studying the writings and thought of Gadādhara gives a window into another modernity; one that has a distinct origin and historical trajectory from that of the modern West. In addition to its explicitly philosophical content, Gadhādhara's writings broadcast a certain mindset—distinctive of those philosophers who called themselves the "moderns" [navyāḥ]—which was characterized by a critical engagement with received tradition and a new found faith in the possibility of progress. Part of a broader trend of "modernism" that permeated south asian culture not just in philosophy but in art and literature, economic structures, political organization, and forms of religiousity, this "other" modernity shares features with modernity in the west but also has its own distinctive character and specificity. In the current post-colonial context, learning about this South Asian modernity-before-modernity is especially helpful in challenging the otherwise easy assumption that modernity is constitutively Western and opening our eyes to other possibilities for what it might mean to be "modern" today.
