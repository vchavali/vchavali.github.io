# Some Reflections on Sellars

## Introduction

In Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind, Wilfrid Sellars offers a mythical genealogy of our concept of knowledge. Beginning with the behaviourist starting point of our "Rylean" ancestors, Sellars traces a gradual course of expansion of our conceptual vocabulary from describing overt speech to episodes of "inner" speech-thoughts-and then to inner non-linguistic "perceptual" episodes.[^1] The basic premise underlying this movement is the idea that human beings are rational creatures. When we observe a person engaging in complex and intentional activity, we observe that this behaviour submits to two distinct forms of explanation. First, we can give a causal account of the actions of the person as proceeding from such and such chemical processes and physiological drives. This is the form of explanation common to all natural objects. Persons can, in addition, give an accounting of themselves from their own perspective-offer their reasons for the actions they engage in. This accounting, in turn, depends for its capacity to inform action on the possibility of it being "guided" by states of consciousness in perceptual episodes.[^2]

In his reading of this Sellarsian story, McDowell pushes back against the idea that the guidedness of perceptual judgment is explained in terms of its relationship to nonconceptual mental content. Under McDowell's account, it is the object itself which guides perceptual judgment in the case of veridical perception. And in the case of nonveridical perception the supposed guidedness of perceptual conceptualization is a mere illusion. In this paper, I offer a vindication of the role of nonconceptual content in Sellars' account of perceptual judgment. However, I suggest that the way to understand the guidedness of perceptual concepts by nonconceptual states cannot be, as Sellars' suggests, in terms of an analogical correspondence between the logical structure of concepts and the structure of sense impressions in an intuition[^3]. By focusing my analysis specifically on nonrepresentational sensory states such as pain, I argue that the guidedness of sensory conceptualization of pain states lies in the way the application of pain concepts in practical reasoning depends on our understanding of them as specifically _phenomenological_ concepts. Notably, the phenomenology of pain is not to be understood as justifying the application of pain concepts. Rather, phenomenological properties of mental states are _postulated_-not apperceived-in order to make intelligible the role that pain concepts play in ethical judgments.

## Freedom in thought and conceptualization

A person's explanation of their own behaviour in terms of reasons differs in an important way from causal explanation. Whereas causal explanation can be judged as "true" or "false", reasons are judged in terms of their appropriateness as "good" or "bad". Reasons are governed by norms of reasoning and reasoning acts are judged against those norms. So, when we describe a person as engaged in successful reason giving activity, whether overt or internal, we characterize them as knowers[^4]. In so doing, we characterize them in normative terms. For this reason, Sellars argues, epistemic characterizations of persons are irreducible to naturalistic characterizations. Naturalistic descriptions place persons in a space of causes. Epistemic descriptions place them in a space of reasons. Conflation of the two represents the epistemological analogue to the naturalistic fallacy in ethics[^5].

While our first encounter with the giving and taking of reasons occurs in episodes of overt speech, on reflection we find that there is the operation of reasons in a person's reasonable behaviour even where their expression is not overt. This leads us to the concept of "thoughts" as inner episodes of reasoning a person engages in when entering into some considered course of action.[^6] Insofar as these inner episodes exemplify acts of reasoning they submit to epistemic description-as, e.g., states of "knowledge" or "judgement". However, insofar as the possession of these states impinges on a person's activity as a natural object, these inner episodes must also submit to naturalistic description as occupying a place in the space of causes. This causal impingement occurs in two directions. In the forward direction, thoughts cause actions. In the reverse, thoughts must themselves be caused by objects in the environment. Otherwise, our epistemic lives would be unconstrained by the natural objects towards which the actions they are intended to direct are oriented. This is a line of thinking that Sellars diagnoses as leading "from Hegel's Phenomenology to nineteenth-century idealism."[^7] In McDowell's (1998) reading, Hegel is Sellars' "bogeyman." Hegel's failure to account for how thought is constrained by the world that is thought "of" makes no sense of the idea that knowledge "pictures" the world.[^8]

However, again following McDowell, this characterization of thoughts as constrained by the world conflicts with another aspect of thinking that Sellars draws from Kant: that thinking is an exercise of our freedom. It is an essential part of our characterization of knowledge as normative that a person can be subject to criticism on the basis of their thinking. And this is only possible if we understand thinking to be an activity a person freely engages in.[^9]

The problem then is as follows: what it means for us to undertake an action freely is for us to understand our intention to act as stemming from reasons which we accept and hold ourselves accountable to. Where the action undertaken is itself the action of thinking, this would imply that we think and judge on the basis of reasons. However, if all of our judgments are explainable in terms of the reasons we have to hold them, then it remains unclear in what sense our judgments can be also constrained by external objects. After all, these objects are wholly natural objects. They can causally impinge on our judgments, but they can never act as reasons for judging.

## Perception as a distinct form of judgment

Sellars answers this dilemma by introducing a form of judgment that exists as an intermediate between thinking and modes of information processing explainable in purely naturalistic terms. This form of judgment is perceptual judgment. It is exemplified most characteristically in overt verbal reports where we proclaim on seeing, e.g., a cat on a mat, "that is a cat on a mat!" When this report is "internal", we have a perceptual judgment. Such a judgment is "thought"-like in the sense of containing a claim about the world.[^10] It possesses a truth value, and it can occur as a premise in an episode of reasoning. As such, an explanation of the epistemic function of perceptual episodes appears to involve both natural and epistemic concepts.

In a certain sense, all epistemic states submit to a two-fold description. After all, epistemic states must also be natural states in order to causally impinge on behaviour. However, Sellars seems to suggest that there is something distinctive about how the naturalistic characterization of perceptual states is involved in descriptions of their epistemic properties.[^11] Namely, Sellars argues that that the conceptualization of perceptual states which render them into judgments is "guided" in some sense by their naturalistic properties.[^12]

Clearly, we cannot understand this notion of "guidance" to be rational in nature. The natural properties of a mental state cannot play a justificatory role in their subsequent conceptualization. However, if the form of guidance involved is just causal, it remains difficult to explain how natural properties of perceptual states differ in their "guiding" role from non-perceptual states of judging. After all, the latter are also natural states whose functional properties depend for their instantiation on the causal properties of the instantiating natural objects.

However, Sellars appears to insist that there is a difference insofar as the properties that guide the conceptual form of perceptual judgements must be states of consciousness.[^13] An initial motivation for this idea comes from Sellars' analysis of perceptual error.[^14] Consider the case of a child learning to employ color language. When presented with a red object, the child is taught to say "red". If, when presented with a green object, the child also says "red"; the nature of this error can be understood in two ways. In the first instance, it may be a failure in the child's use of the color concept "red". That is, the child may have failed to recognize that the color concept "red" is meant to exclude incompatible color concepts such as "green". However, it is also possible that the error does not lie with the child's understanding of color concepts. Rather, it may be that they could not distinguish, at the sensory level, between the objects' colors. In other words, the child could be red-green color blind. The difference between these two notions of error is significant. In the first instance, the error is attributable to the child as a rational (or proto-rational) agent. The child is at fault. In the second case, however, the failure is not attributable to the child as agent. The child is not at fault. They just have a differently configured sensory apparatus.

The ability to distinguish between sensory and rational error with regards to perceptual judgement requires us to recognize a shared basis of conceptualization between "true" and "ostensive" perceptual episodes. This being so, because the guidedness of conceptualization by sensation is the same, the child cannot be held responsible for a mistake in their autonomous deployment of conceptual faculties. As McDowell puts it, it is because true and ostensible perceptions contain "the same claim" that the error cannot be attributed to the conceptualization of that perception.[^15] Yet, it remains unclear why the guidedness of conceptual perceptual judgements must lie in their relationship to states of consciousness. McDowell gives the memorable example of retinal activation states as equally capable of explaining the sensory basis for true and ostensive perceptions containing the "same claim"[^16].

## Kant on Intuitions

In Science and Metaphysics (1968), Sellars offers an explanation for the peculiar guiding role of sensory consciousness in perceptual judgment through a reading of the Kantian notion of intuition as sensory representation. For Kant, knowledge arises out of the operation of two principal faculties: sensibility and the understanding.[^17] The faculty of sensibility explains our receptivity to sensory impressions. The understanding explains our capacity for representing objects through concepts. The understanding possesses the capacity for the spontaneous generation of concepts. However, at least for finite minds, it is only possible to know objects via the cooperation of these two faculties. This is because, without concepts, we have no way of forming judgments regarding our sensory representations of objects. And without the sensibility, there is no way to explain how the free operation of our sensory capacities comes to be constrained by objects. As Kant explains, we receive sensory representations of objects from the sensibility and then we know these objects by bringing these representations under the purview of concepts.[^18]

However, Sellars identifies a certain tension in the Kantian picture. Representation is a normative concept and appears, therefore, to be a conceptual entity at the onset. How, then, are we to understand the idea that there can be sensory representations of objects that are the products of "sheer receptivity"[^19], prior to the involvement of the understanding? Central to this problem is the ambiguous status of the Kantian notion of intuitions. Intuitions, in their basic sense, are the products of sensibility. However, unlike simple sense impressions, intuitions have formal properties.

Sellars sees two senses of the word intuition in Kant. In the first instance, we have intuitions as representations of sensory manifolds. In the second instance, we have intuitions just as sensory manifolds, themselves unrepresented as such. The former notion, which Sellars connects to our capacity for imagination,[^20] does involve the operation of the understanding. Sellars characterizes intuitions, in this sense, as concepts pertaining to an individual as a "this-such". The second notion is characterized by "sheer receptivity". It is formal in the sense that it is the structuring of sense impressions into a manifold. But it is not represented as such; and is therefore free of concepts.[^21]

There are, then, four stages to the formation of fully fledged perceptual judgements. First, we receive sensory impressions which are structured into manifolds of sensation. Next, these manifolds come to be understood as manifolds-as "this-suches."[^22] These representations of manifolds are then brought under general concepts as "red" and "square", etc. Finally, these conceptual-sensory complexes are rendered into judgments as "this is a red square" by bringing them under a grammatical structure.[^23]

Our original question then, rendered in this new Kantian language is as follows: what role do these nonconceptual intuitions-manifolds of sensation not represented as such-play in "guiding" the subsequent particular and general conceptualizations? Sellars' insight is that where objects can be conceptualized in terms of, e.g., color properties in spatial relations to one another, there are corresponding concepts applicable to the sense impressions involved in the intuition received from that object. For instance: where we perceive a pyramid as having a red tip and a blue base, we conceptualize the corresponding intuition of that pyramid as involving a "blue" impression as occurring in a corresponding relation to a "red" impression. Here, the notion of correspondence involved is that of analogy.[^24] The conceptualization of the pyramid as given in fully fledged perceptual judgments are guided by representations of sensory manifolds, which are themselves guided by structural relationships between sensory impressions within that manifold just in the sense that at each stage the concepts involved must correspond by analogy to concepts involved in characterizing the objects at the lower representational levels. And ultimately, for these representations to be representations of the object, they must correspond to analogous concepts applicable to the object qua object. With this picture in place, we have an explanation for the difference in problems of sensation vs errors of judgment as in the case of the child learning to distinguish between red and green objects.

## The sense impression inference

Sellars, in what he calls the "sense impression inference"[^25] argues that it is necessary to postulate the existence of sensory impressions capable of being structured into intuitions because otherwise we cannot explain the guidedness of veridical perception in the following sense: Veridical perception involves the conceptualization of an object possessing such and such features as possessing those features. Part of what it means to correctly conceptualize is to say that the conceptualization of an object in perception is systematically explained by facts about that object in virtue of which the conceptualization can be considered both true and generated by the object.[^26] Sellars seems to suggest, however, that for us to explain the systematicity of conceptualization-as opposed to just the systematicity of behavioural discrimination-we must postulate the existence of "immediate non-conceptual occasions" which perceptual concepts correspond to[^27]. And, Sellars suggests, these must "surely be construed as states of the perceiver".

The example of learning color concepts is leveraged to elucidate this point. The fact that learned color concepts are transmitted by training from teacher to student implies that the learned conceptualization must be postulated to proceed from "cues which systematically correspond to color and shape attribute families."[^28] Why must the basis for conceptualization be analogical correspondence? In particular, why can't causal relations between perceptual states and perceived objects, themselves, do this work? The key idea seems to be in the need for an explanation of the systematicity of conceptualization across both veridical and nonveridical perceptual judgments.[^29]

The idea is, perhaps, as follows. In cases of nonveridical perception where the fault is attributed to the sensory faculties rather than the inappropriate deployment of concepts, the only way to make this distinction intelligible is to suppose that whatever it is that makes the deployment of the concept not what is incorrect in this case must be the same as what explains the correctness of concept deployment in the veridical case. Otherwise, we lack the resources to explain the difference between a failure of conceptualization and a failure of sensation. The problem is especially acute with respect to hallucinations, where there is no object at all with respect to which we can explain the shared basis for conceptualization between the hallucination and the veridical perception. So, to explain the correctness of conceptualization in both cases, we postulate the existence of an "immediate non-conceptual occasion" that is shared between both cases and in analogy to which the conceptualization occurring in perceptual judgements proceeds.

## Two problems with the sense impression inference

Note that this way of framing the sense-impression inference does seem to immunize Sellars' somewhat from the criticism leveraged by McDowell.[^30] In particular, under McDowell's disjunctivism about perceptual illusions, the sense in which the illusion and veridical perception "contain the same claim" is just that they have the same logical structure. However, only in the case of veridical perception is there an analogous structuring at the level of intuition-where, for McDowell the structuring that is the intuition is not a structuring of nonconceptual mental content, but rather the structuring of the object itself. In a sense, the fact that for Sellars, it is only the "form" and not the "matter" of intuition that explains the guidedness of conceptualization is what lends itself to this form of disjunctivism. Even so, it is not clear that McDowell has the resources on his account to account for the correctness of ostensible perceptual judgments qua conceptualization, contra ostensible perceptual judgments whose failure lies in the deployment of concepts. Of course, McDowell is still free to simply deny that such a distinction is intelligible-though the viability of that strategy in the face of the facts of concept acquisition remains uncertain.

However, even conceding the point to Sellars' account, it is not so clear that Sellars' has accomplished his goal of demonstrating that the structuring involved in intuition is the structuring of states of consciousness. If by consciousness we mean, for instance, the sorts of states that separate forms of information processing carried out by animals with central nervous systems from those of such things as plants and sea anemone. Again, the point is that the analogy involved in guiding sensory conceptualization is an analogy of structure. Nothing is presupposed about the matter of intuition as sensory manifold in the formulation of the sense impression inference. Certainly, even the sensory processes of plants and sea anemone involve their entering into structured informational states that obey structural analogies to sensed objects in order for them to operate computationally on them. Or consider the case of image categorization software which take images of objects and output linguistic expressions representing their spatial features (e.g. as "square", "circular", etc.). Here, in discriminating the shapes of objects placed in front of a camera, it is necessary for the device to convert optical information obtained from the camera into transistor activation patterns in silica such that relations between elements of the transistor array obtain which are analogous in some way to (1) spatial relations of the final object representation and (2) the object-level analogue to spatial representations at the level of the natural object in causal dependence on which the final linguistic report is generated. Where an error in the linguistic output of the device is explainable in terms of a problem with the camera, it is reasonable to suppose that the in silica representation qua picture has its structure in common with successful cases. Yet, it is not clear that this in silica structure counts as a state of consciousness.

## A Rehabilitation of Sense Impressions as States of Consciousness

Are we then forced to give up on the Sellarsian idea that intuitions are states of consciousness? I do not think so. Rather, let us reconsider the notion that intuition necessarily guides conceptual representation via possessing an analogy of structure. As a matter of fact, such a notion is not even intelligible with regards to certain forms of perceptual conceptualizations: namely, the forms of sensibility that Sellars refers to as "bodily sensations" and "feelings."[^31] Unlike the case of visual intuitions, where it is natural to conceive of the component sense impressions as structured into space-like relations, such a conception of structuring is not readily apparent in sensations of, e.g., pain and nausea. Moreover, as I will argue shortly, pain and nausea cannot be understood as representational (even representational qua pictures) of any object-level properties.

Nonetheless, they are capable of being rendered conceptually in judgments. Otherwise, pain states could not be attributed to individuals in sentences such as, "that person is in pain". And such attributions where they are directed towards oneself (as in judgements of the form, "I am in pain") readily submit to the same sort of analysis that led, in the first place, to the postulation of perceptual judgments directed towards external objects. After all, we regularly observe linguistically competent adults attributing pain to themselves systematically and without the apparent mediation of inferences when certain kinds of sensory stimuli are applied to them, much the same way as they attribute shape and color to objects in the presence of the appropriate visual stimuli.

Note the emphasis on linguistic competence. Indeed, pain attributions are trained behaviours in precisely the same way as color and shape attributions. This being the case, the possibility of concept acquisition with regards to pain is just as much in need of explanation as color, etc. When a child is learning pain concepts, they are taught to attribute pain to themselves in certain circumstances and not in others. And just as with color attribution, it is possible for children to attribute pain concepts to themselves in circumstances where pain attribution would not ordinarily be observed. In this case, like with the case of color attribution, we are faced with at least two possible explanations. The first is that there is a misattribution of pain due to an erroneous use of pain concepts. The second is that there is a misattribution not explainable as a failure of conceptualization.

The latter possibility, however, can be discarded at the onset. The only way attribution of a sensory concept could be classified as a mistake in this case would be if the concept is applied to an intuition that is structured appropriately for that conceptualization but fails to picture the object towards which the resulting judgement is directed. However, in the case of pain, there is no corresponding notion of a structured sensory manifold picturing an object.

Nonetheless, it does seem like misattribution of pain concepts is possible. For instance, in the mistaken attribution of pain to an actor on stage. Note that the attribution of pain to others-though not conceptualized as proceeding directly from sensation-must involve the same concept as that involved in self-attribution, since it plays the same functional role in both cases. This being the case, the correct attribution of pain concepts to oneself in the presence of the appropriate sensory stimulus remains in need of explanation, just as in the case of outward directed perceptions.

In the following sections, I will argue that (1) sense impressions associated with pain must be postulated to explain certain features of pain discourse that are otherwise unintelligible and (2) the sense impressions so postulated have features in virtue of which we can explain the "guidedness" of pain concepts in self-attributions of pain.

Before we proceed, however, there is a line of argumentation that must be considered in response to the above claim that misattribution of pain concepts cannot fail to be conceptual. Namely, a sensory manifold is not necessary to explain failure as a problem of sensory picturing. Rather, all that is necessary is that a certain sensation occurs, ordinarily, in the presence of certain causes. In that case, though the sensation is not structurally complex, it could still be understood as picturing the state of affairs that ordinarily causes it. The issue with this response is that, as shall be argued shortly, pain concepts are not concepts that represent natural objects. Rather, they are concepts that represent experiences. They are conceptually phenomenological. This is not to claim that they are associated with sensations (understood as non-conceptual states) as a matter of their phenomenology. It is merely to say that they are concepts of phenomenology. They are concepts the attribution of which represents an object as a phenomenological object-just as concepts such as 'cause' and 'effect' are conceptualizations of objects as natural objects and concepts such as 'premise' and 'conclusion' are conceptualizations of objects as rational objects. This is so far just a claim, the argument for which is yet to be formulated. This argument has three parts. First, we will show that pain concepts cannot be assimilated to rational concepts. Second, we will show that they cannot be assimilated to naturalistic concepts. Third, we will show that the attribution of pain concepts cannot be governed by a learned rule.

## Pain concepts cannot be assimilated to epistemic concepts

Our analysis of pain begins by observing that pain attributions are value laden, in at least the following sense: Attributing the concept "pain" to a creature allows us to infer that it is in a "bad" state. Here, by "bad" we mean at a minimum that this attribution to a state of affairs supports an inference in an episode of practical reasoning to an "ought to alleviate" regarding that state of affairs. In other words, attributions of badness by a subject involve them in ethical obligations as a part of their understanding of the import of such attributions.

While such ethical import as arising from pain concept attribution are general in character, they are especially transparent in the domain of medical ethics. Consider, the use of sedative analgesics such as opioid agonists in medical and veterinary practice. There are two fundamental ethical considerations operative in the use of such medicines. First, the principle of _beneficence_ suggests that the medical professional has an obligation to use medications in accordance with their clinical indication where they would benefit their patient. In the case of opioid medications, the clinical indication is to alleviate pain. On the other hand, insofar as the use of opioids exposes a patient to significant potential harm, their administration to patients not in pain would be unethical. Because it contravenes the obligation of a physician to avoid unnecessary harm (_nonmaleficence_).

Now, if a patient's being in pain were an epistemic state of that patient or depended causally or conceptually on their being in a certain epistemic state; then patients that are not capable of robust linguistic behaviour, such as prelinguistic infants, and those that are nonverbal due profound traumatic brain injury, dementia, or developmental disorders, could not be in pain states. Also, nonverbal animals could not be in pain states. However, there are cases where it is ethical for a physician to administer opioid medications to patients who are nonverbal. Also, it can be ethical to administer opiate medications in veterinary medicine. Therefore, it cannot be the case that pain is an epistemic state of that patient or depends causally or conceptually on their being in a certain epistemic state.

## Pain concepts cannot be assimilated to naturalistic concepts

In demonstrating the irreducibility of pain concepts to naturalistic concepts we will consider two possible ways such a reduction could proceed. The first, and most easily discardable, is that pain concepts are just behavioural concepts. In other words, to attribute pain to a person is just to attribute certain behaviours or behavioural dispositions to that person. However, we note that sometimes the administration of opioid medications is justified even in the absence of pain behaviours including the verbalization of pain as well as nonverbal behaviour such as grimacing, etc., conventionally associated with pain states. For instance, analgesics and sedatives are regularly employed when administering paralytic agents. If we assume that the practice of giving analgesics and sedatives to awake but paralysed patients such as those undergoing endotracheal intubation is medically justified on the grounds that such patients still experience pain, then we must conclude that pain attribution is not reducible to the attribution of behavioural concepts.

On the other hand, we might analyse pain concepts in causal terms as, for instance, those mental states that are caused by and register tissue damage under ordinary physiologic conditions. In this case, it would never be ethical (barring other clinical contraindications) to withhold opiates from patients undergoing tissue damage who have intact pain receptors and at least some intact pain sensory and integration pathways. However, this would not explain the reduction in dose and frequency of opioid use in patients with central alterations in pain processing associated with increased pain threshold as compared to patients with baseline central pain processing in situations with comparable degrees of tissue damage. In the case of advanced frontotemporal dementia for instance, where there is neurophysiologic evidence of altered pain threshold and tolerance,[^32] the valuation of benefit and harm with respect to opioid use may differ from that of patients without this condition. Similar considerations may dictate alterations of opioid use in patients with high-level cervical spinal injuries.

Empirical details aside, it seems at least possible that in cases of radical alterations to central pain processing the use of opioid medications may no longer confer their intended benefit. In these cases, the withholding of opiate analgesics is specifically justified by a belief that the patient is not experiencing pain. That is to say, the withholding of opioids in these cases is justified by a belief that specifically those parts of the pain sensation-integration system responsible for generating pain experience are inoperative, despite the functioning of other parts of the neuronal apparatus responsible for sensing and processing tissue damage and other physiologic causes of pain. Otherwise, if all there is to pain attribution is the causal processes involved in detecting and responding to tissue damage; insofar as some elements of pain processing such as peripheral receptors and certain autonomic responses to pain stimuli may remain intact, it is unclear why central processing of pain is of special ethical import.

## The attribution of pain concepts cannot be governed by a learned rule

In the prior sections we have shown that pain concepts cannot be analysed into causal or epistemic concepts. It is also not the case that the application of pain concepts to certain mental states and subsequent inferences such as "X ought to be alleviated because it is a pain state" finds its ultimate justification in an explicitly learned rule, such as in the case of our conceptualization of a chess position as "threatening a piece". Otherwise, given that administering sedating analgesics such as opioid agonists has potential for causing serious harm, we would have to accept that the rule to avoid unnecessary harm can be superseded by a learned rule that has no deeper justification than such things as the rules of a game or conventional rules of social etiquette. This is implausible. Therefore, our acceptance of the rule "If X is a pain state then we ought to alleviate it" must be itself be underwritten by a sort of justification that is at least capable of outweighing the foundational medical-ethical principle of nonmaleficence.

## A sense-impression inference for pain concepts

In the above three sections, we have shown that the attribution of pain, even where it is inferred on the basis of linguistic reports, nonverbal behaviours, or physiologic properties of the individual, cannot be reduced to epistemic, behavioural, or naturalistic states of that individual. That is to say, the appropriateness of the pain attribution cannot be understood either as the appropriateness of a judgement or the appropriateness of a picture. Additionally, pain attributions form an ineliminable part of medical-ethical discourse. Otherwise, the application of medications such as morphine, with a very high potential for harm, could never be justified on medical-ethical grounds.

Rather, our justification for using harmful medications depends on our understanding certain states of organisms to be sufficiently "bad" as to counterbalance the harm that could otherwise ensue. And this understanding proceeds from our knowledge (to use the traditional idiom) that what it is "like" to be in that state is bad-i.e. that the relevant pain concept must be a distinctly phenomenological concept. In other words, the attribution of pain to others is intimately connected with pain self-attribution. When we attribute pain to others, we bring them under the same form of description that we understand as guiding the attribution of pain to ourselves. We take the other to be in a state "like" that in which we find ourselves when we self-attribute pain.

Moreover, there is a close relationship between attributions of pain and attributions of pain beliefs. Where we understand a person to have competency in the use of pain concepts, judging that a person believes themselves to be in pain is adequate reason to infer that they are in pain. Importantly, the resulting judgment that the person is in pain involves the same pain concept-in terms of the inferences it licences-as is involved in attributions of pain to nonverbal humans and to nonhuman animals. Given that pain concepts are not synonymous with pain belief concepts (as has been argued), the possibility of inferring pain experience from pain belief in cases of self-attribution implies that self-attributions of pain concepts (1) correspond systematically to some condition of correctness for such attributions, and (2) this condition of correctness is shared between concept-possessing and concept-lacking creatures. What does this correctness condition consist in? Whatever it is, it must be a condition that is satisfiable by mental states and is explicable neither in terms of naturalistic properties nor epistemic properties of those states.

## Pain concepts as phenomenological concepts

We began this essay by arguing that a person's behaviour submits to two distinct forms of description-one external and the other internal. In the first instance, we have naturalistic description that features in causal explanation of a person's behaviour as the behaviour of a natural object. In the second instance, we have epistemic descriptions that feature in rational explanations of a person's behaviour as the behaviour of a free agent. As a consequence of this second form of description, we postulate the existence of a class of mental states possessed by rational agents that can be characterized in epistemic terms as states of judgement and knowledge.

To attribute pain to an individual, however, is to understand them as the possessor of a mental state that is subject to a peculiar form of characterization. This form of characterization, as has been argued, is neither a causal characterization nor an epistemic one. Therefore, we must postulate the existence of a third category of concepts distinct from epistemic and natural concepts-what we have called "phenomenological" concepts. And we must postulate a third category of mental states-experiential states-such that they are shared between conceptual and nonconceptual creatures and subject not just to naturalistic description but description in terms of what they are "like". Furthermore, this third category of mental states "guide" conceptualizations and self-attributions of pain in the sense that (1) they explain the systematic correspondence of pain belief with pain experience and (2) they explain the correctness of pain self-attribution by competent users of pain concepts.
