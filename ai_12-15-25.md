# Can Generative AI be Creative?

## Historical background

Alphago's 2016 games against Lee Sedol have been widely acclaimed not just as highly skilled play but artful play, instances of beauty and creativity on the Go board[^1]. Eight years later, reflecting on the impact of AI on Go playing, Lee Sedol suggests in a lecture at Seoul University, that while AI plays with a high win-rate, it cannot play "masterful games". Elaborating the point, Sedol argues that the most creative part of the game is not the play but the review, where the players discuss the reasons for the moves they make[^2]. With AI, however, there is no exchange of reasons beyond that a given move yields the highest win rate. Yet, if yielding the highest chances of victory does not count, as Lee Sedol appears to suggest, as an adequate reason for a move's being masterful; then the question remains: What does it mean to play creatively?

The question of creativity in deep learning systems has only deepened over the past decade, with the development of such projects as DeepMind's WaveNet[^3], for voice and music generation, and DeepDream[^4], for image generation from prompts. And since the release, of publicly available tools such as Midjourney[^5] and Stable Diffusion[^6], the relevance of these questions to economics, culture, and law have become even more acute. Does AI generated content represent a form of creative output? If so, who is to be credited as the artist: The user? The corporate service provider? The AI entity itself?

In the cultural space, answers to these questions determine, for instance, the eligibility of AI generated submissions to art festivals, contests, and public awards. In the legal and economic spaces, the applicability of, e.g., copyright law to AI generated content may also hinge on whether AI output represents a form of creative expression[^7].

## Towards an analysis of creativity

To make progress on the question of whether the output of generative AI can be considered creative or artful, it is necessary to offer a conceptual analysis of creativity. While the formulation of this analysis must be constrained by the way in which actual attributions of creativity are made, it cannot be so narrowly construed as to definitionally exclude all non-human productivity. At the same time, an analysis of creativity cannot be so broad as to include those effects of purely mechanistic or natural causal processes regarding which attributions of creativity are uncontroversially withheld.

Consider, for instance, an early formulation of the concept of creativity in Stein (1953):

>The creative work is a novel work that is accepted as tenable or useful or satisfying by a group in some point in time[^8].

Here, we note two distinct dimensions involved in attributing creativity to a work. First is a novelty criterion. For a work to be creative it most not have existed before in the form in which it exists now. The second is a value criterion. The work must be judged by the community as valuable in some sense: "as tenable, useful, or satisfying".

The problem with this proposal, however, is that it extends attributions of creativity to natural artifacts. Consider the contrast between a natural rock formation and a sculpture. Certainly, there are many natural formations that see large swathes of visitors marvelling at their beauty and splendour. Yet, to characterize the geologic processes that went into their production as creative acts seems to be an overreach of the plain meaning of the term.

## Creative intent as an additional constraint

Klausen (2010) makes some progress on the question by introducing a notion of creative intent into the analysis[^9]. For Klausen, the motivation is that if the onus were on the community to judge value and novelty, then it would render unrecognized creative output impossible. However, if novelty and value are attributed to the product based on the creator's own intent, then the need for societal recognition may be circumvented[^10]. Additionally, such an intention condition does appear to provide resources for distinguishing between natural causal processes and genuine creative acts.

Reference to creative intent in the analysis of creativity represents a genuine insight. But the proposal as laid out by Klausen remains problematic on at least two counts. In the first instance, it is unclear that the problem of unrecognized creativity is adequately answered by reference to creative intent. In the second instance, it is unclear that artistic creativity can be meaningfully distinguished from other forms of creativity under this proposal.

## Creative intent does not solve the problem of unrecognized creativity

With regards to the first problem, there are two ways to read the role of creative intent in the ascertainment of an artifact's value and novelty. Either (1) the intent to create something of novelty and value is necessary and sufficient for attributing such to the resulting artifact; or (2) the intent is necessary but not sufficient for the attribution.

In the first instance, if intent were sufficient for being creative then it would be impossible to fail to be creative. But insofar as creativity represents a substantive normative category, it is necessary that there be meaningful failure criteria regarding creative acts. Otherwise, the category qua normative category would be vacuous. If, on the other hand, creative intent were merely necessary but not sufficient for attributions of creativity; then, insofar as community appraisal would still be necessary for ascertainment of value and novelty, unrecognized creative output would remain an impossibility.

## Creativity in art requires a distinct analysis

Additionally, the hitherto discussed analyses of creativity do not provide resources to draw substantive distinctions between different forms of creativity. Consider the case, discussed at the onset, regarding attributions of creativity with respect to Go players, human or AI. For there to be a meaningful difference between merely competent play and play that is creative in a specifically aesthetic sense, it must be possible to distinguish between creativity that is purely instrumental from creativity oriented towards aesthetic ends. After all, even competent play frequently involves ideas that are novel and produce value with respect to the goals of play.

An obvious strategy for distinguishing between aesthetic and instrumental creativity is to draw the distinction at the level of the value aimed at and produced. Paraphrasing Klausen's initial proposal for attributions of creativity in general:

A product is judged to be creative iff

1\. It is judged to be novel  
2\. It is judged to have value  
3\. It is produced specifically with the intent to satisfy criteria (1) and (2).

To amend these criteria to narrowly specify artistic creativity, we could modify criterion (2) as follows:

2\*. It is judged to have aesthetic value

Where aesthetic value is understood to mean such things as beauty, etc.

The problem with this formulation is that in cases where the intent to create something beautiful is itself motivated by ulterior strategic considerations; that the creativity exhibited by the product is specifically aesthetic in nature would be merely contingent. However, insofar as artistic creativity is understood to be a distinct class of creative acts or products, that an instance of creativity is aesthetic rather than, e.g., instrumental should be an essential feature of that form of creativity.

Take, for instance, a scenario where a construction firm is approached by a client to design an office building. Suppose that the firm is competing with other firms for the commission and must produce a design that is maximally pleasing to the client. The construction of this design would exhibit a mode of creativity such that the value criterion takes the form:

2\*\*. It is judged to be most pleasing to the client

In such a case, the form that the end product's value takes will depend on features of the client. For instance, if the client values cost-efficiency above all else, then the resulting product would be maximally cost-efficient. If the client values beauty, then beauty would also feature as a design value. However, in either case, whether or not beauty features as a value would be a contingent fact about the product: In both cases-where the client values or does not value beauty-the resulting product would be creative. And, insofar as the exhibition of creativity would result from the same underlying intention and overall strategy involved in the design; it would be odd to say that the kind of creativity involved in each instance is essentially different.

## Creativity as a form of intelligence

The lesson to draw from this is as follows: the fundamental constraint in formulating an adequate analysis of artistic creativity is that it must provide resources for distinguishing instances where aesthetic value is produced incidentally as a side-effect of basically non-aesthetic processes from cases where aesthetic value is connected in an essential way to the attribution of creativity. Such an analysis would exclude cases where beauty is produced via a wholly natural process, due to mere serendipity, or as incidental to ulterior strategic considerations.

In working out an adequate analysis, we start by recognizing artistic activity as essentially the activity of an intelligent agent. Artwork is subjectable to critical appraisal. And a constitutive part of such appraisal involves the asking of "why?" regarding the various transformations an artist imposes upon a medium. For instance, in the construction of a poem, the same semantic content can be expressed in a myriad of ways. However, the specificity of a particular poem lies in the choices the poet makes. And asking why the poet chooses, e.g., one word rather than another is an essential part of the critical reception of a poem. Therefore, it is natural to understand creativity as involving intelligent, reason-giving activity.

Intelligence is, in turn, an exercise of an agent's autonomy in directing their epistemic faculties to the solution of problems pertaining to the agent's own needs. As Montemayor (2023) argues, in the absence of agential autonomy there is no principled way to distinguish between information processing, broadly construed, and the specific form of information processing characteristic of minds[^11]. What separates properly intelligent activity from nonintelligent information processing is that the former is attributable to the agent as a unity, rather than to some subset of its parts. And this attribution is itself based on the activity so construed being oriented towards needs that are themselves attributable to the agent[^12]. In the case of aesthetic activity, the needs it orients itself toward must be aesthetic needs as pertaining to the agent themselves. In other words, it is necessary that the aesthetic value being generated is valuable as aesthetic value to the agent.

Moreover, insofar as the aesthetic needs pertain to the agent, the problem of unrecognized creativity meets with a straightforward solution. An unrecognized creative act is still creative insofar as it satisfies the aesthetic needs of the artist. The charge of normative vacuity, on the other hand, is blocked by recognizing that the artist has aesthetic needs only in virtue of their being embedded in an artistic community which provides the intersubjective grounding for the value the product instantiates.

## An analysis of artistic creativity

In sum, we offer the following analysis of artistic creativity:

For a product to be an instance of artistic creativity it must satisfy the following two criteria:

(1) Its production must involve substantive transformations of some media directly attributable to the decision-making of an intelligent agent

(2) In effecting the transformations of the media, the agent stipulated in (1) must act for specifically aesthetic reasons where such reasons are understood to pertain to the agent's own aesthetic needs.

Here, we will refer to criterion (1) as the originality criterion. And to (2) as the intelligence criterion.

The intelligence criterion addresses the constraint that creativity must be the action of an intelligent agent connected in an essential way to the production of aesthetic value. The originality criterion represents a reworking of the novelty criterion from Stein (1953). Consider, for instance, the argument put forth in _Feist v. Rural (1991)_ regarding originality as pertaining to copyright law. In that case, the court argues that if two poets independently create the same poem, both instances of the poem should count as original contributions, though neither is novel[^13]. Additionally, the notion of "substantiveness" serves to distinguish creativity from forms of decision-making which pertain to the aesthetic needs of an agent but are not artistic as such. In particular, it serves to block the overextension of the term "creative" to acts of commissioning, appreciating, and appraising works of art.

## Artistic creativity in generative AI output

We are now equipped to address the question of whether content produced by generative AI algorithms constitute artistic creativity. In particular, we will consider the output of AI services such as Midjourney which produce content from textual prompts and software such as Katago which act to optimize specific metrics such as win-rate in Go. I argue that the transformations performed by such generative AI service in producing some output do not, themselves, constitute artistic activity because they cannot be attributable as artistic activity either to (1) the AI service understood either as a corporation or as a piece of software or (2) the user who provides the prompt.

The transformations cannot be attributable as artistic activity to the AI service because they fail to satisfy the intelligence criterion on creative acts. Insofar as an AI service acts purely to satisfy the aesthetic needs of the user in its generation of content, it does not seek to satisfy any aesthetic needs specifically attributable to it. Therefore, such a service cannot satisfy the intelligence criterion.

Moreover, the transformations cannot be attributable as artistic activity to the user because they fail to satisfy the originality criterion on creative acts. Insofar as the actual decision-making on the part of the user is limited to the construction of the prompt, any transformations performed after the prompt is inputted into the AI and prior to the user receiving the output from it cannot be directly attributed to the user. This being the case, the production of the output can only be attributed as a creative act to the user if their prompting activity represents a "substantial contribution". Here, what counts as substantial will, ultimately, end up being a matter of history and social convention. Nonetheless, there must be at least some in-principle bounds on this notion of substantivity. Otherwise, it would be impossible to draw such distinctions as between, e.g., a patron commissioning a painting and director of a stage play or film. In a certain sense, the prompting activity of a patron and that of a director both count as artistic acts, insofar as they involve decisions made with reference to aesthetic reasons and needs. But only the director's activity can be considered creative as such because only their activity involves a substantive creative contribution to the final production.
